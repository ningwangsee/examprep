# Chapter 10: Alcohol and Other Drugs
Source: https://www.dps.texas.gov/internetforms/Forms/DL-7.pdf#page=64
(Texas Driver Handbook DL-7, Jan 2026)

## DWI Definition (Texas Penal Code §49.04)
Texas defines **intoxication** as:
1. Having a BAC (blood alcohol concentration) of **0.08% or higher**, OR
2. Not having the **normal use of mental or physical faculties** by reason of alcohol, a drug, a dangerous drug, a controlled substance, or any other substance

Both definitions can be used in prosecution — you can be convicted of DWI with a BAC below 0.08% if a police officer determines you have lost normal use of your faculties.

## BAC Limits

| Driver Type | Legal Limit |
|-------------|-------------|
| Adult driver (21+), non-commercial | 0.08% |
| Commercial driver (CDL) | 0.04% |
| Driver under 21 | Any detectable amount (Zero Tolerance) |

## Zero Tolerance Law (Under 21)
- Texas prohibits **any detectable amount** of alcohol for drivers under 21
- Charge: Driving Under the Influence (DUI) — a Class C misdemeanor (minor alcohol offense)
- Penalties: fine up to $500, 60-day license suspension, 20–40 hours community service, attendance at alcohol awareness class
- Subsequent offenses carry increased penalties including longer suspensions

## Implied Consent Law
- By driving on Texas public roads, you **automatically consent** to chemical testing (breath, blood, or urine) if lawfully arrested for DWI
- Officers may use any lawful method to obtain a specimen
- Refusal triggers Administrative License Revocation (ALR) penalties

## Administrative License Revocation (ALR) Program

| Situation | First Offense | Second+ Offense |
|-----------|---------------|-----------------|
| **Failed** chemical test (BAC at/above legal limit) | 90-day suspension | 1-year suspension |
| **Refused** chemical test | 180-day suspension | 2-year suspension |

- ALR suspension is separate from any criminal DWI penalty
- Arresting officer gives you notice of suspension; you have 15 days to request a hearing
- During 40-day temporary driving permit (DIC-25): you may drive while ALR is pending

## DWI Criminal Penalties

### First Offense DWI
- Class B misdemeanor
- Fine up to **$2,000**
- Jail: 3 days to 180 days (72-hour mandatory minimum)
- License suspension: 90 days to 1 year

### Second Offense DWI
- Class A misdemeanor
- Fine up to **$4,000**
- Jail: 30 days to 1 year
- License suspension: 180 days to 2 years

### Third Offense DWI (and subsequent)
- Third-degree felony
- Fine up to **$10,000**
- Prison: 2 to 10 years
- License suspension: 180 days to 2 years

### DWI with Child Passenger (under 15 in the vehicle)
- State jail felony
- Fine up to **$10,000**
- Jail: 180 days to 2 years

### Intoxication Assault (causing serious bodily injury)
- Third-degree felony: 2–10 years prison; fine up to $10,000

### Intoxication Manslaughter (causing death)
- Second-degree felony: 2–20 years prison; fine up to $10,000

## Effects of Alcohol on Driving

### How Alcohol Impairs Driving
- Slows reaction time
- Reduces coordination and muscle control
- Impairs judgment and decision-making
- Reduces visual acuity and field of vision
- Increases risk-taking behavior
- Causes false confidence

### Factors Affecting BAC
- Body weight: lower weight → higher BAC per drink
- Gender: women typically reach higher BAC than men with the same amount
- Food: eating slows alcohol absorption
- Rate of drinking: faster consumption = faster BAC rise
- Medications: many drugs interact dangerously with alcohol

### Alcohol is NOT eliminated by:
- Coffee, food, or cold water
- Exercise
- Time is the only way to lower BAC; the liver processes about 1 standard drink per hour

## Open Container Law (Texas Transportation Code §49.031)
- It is illegal to have an **open container** of alcohol in the passenger area of a motor vehicle on a public highway
- Applies to all occupants (not just the driver)
- Penalty: Class C misdemeanor, fine up to $500
- Exceptions: passengers in a bus, taxi, limo, or RV living area

## Drugs and Driving
- **Illegal drugs** (marijuana, cocaine, methamphetamine, etc.) impair driving and are prosecuted under DWI laws
- **Prescription drugs** can impair driving; read warning labels; never drive if a medication causes drowsiness, dizziness, or impaired judgment
- **Over-the-counter medications** (antihistamines, cold medicines) can also impair driving
- A person can be convicted of DWI from drug impairment even with a BAC of 0.00%
- Drug Recognition Experts (DRE) are trained officers who can identify drug impairment

## Driver Responsibility Program (historical note)
- Prior to September 1, 2019, Texas assessed annual surcharges on DWI convictions
- DWI: $1,000–$2,000/year for 3 years
- DWI with high BAC (0.16+): $2,000/year for 3 years
- Program was repealed in 2019; existing surcharge debts were eliminated
