# Chapter 8: Speed and Special Situations / Chapter 9: Driving Environments
Source: https://www.dps.texas.gov/internetforms/Forms/DL-7.pdf#page=54
(Texas Driver Handbook DL-7, Jan 2026)

## The Basic Speed Rule
- Always drive at a speed that is **reasonable and prudent** for existing conditions
- Never exceed the posted speed limit
- May need to drive **below** the posted limit in adverse conditions (rain, fog, ice, construction, heavy traffic)
- The posted limit is the **maximum** under ideal conditions — not a target

## Prima Facie (Default Statutory) Speed Limits

| Location | Speed Limit |
|----------|-------------|
| Urban/business/residential district | 30 mph |
| Alley | 15 mph |
| School zone (when children present or lights flashing) | 20 mph (typically; check posted sign) |
| Highway outside urban district — daytime | 70 mph |
| Highway outside urban district — nighttime | 65 mph |
| Numbered county road (rural) | 60 mph |

These are prima facie limits — default when no sign is posted. Posted signs override these defaults.

## Maximum Posted Speed Limits in Texas
- **85 mph** — State Highway 130 (SH-130) toll road between Austin and Seguin (highest in the US)
- **80 mph** — certain rural interstates in West Texas
- **75 mph** — many rural interstates and state highways
- Speed limits are set by TxDOT based on engineering and traffic studies

## Minimum Speed Law
- It is illegal to drive so slowly that you **impede the normal and reasonable flow of traffic** unless reduced speed is necessary for safety
- On freeways, vehicles must keep up with traffic flow unless a hazard requires slower speed

## School Zones
- Observe the reduced speed limit whenever:
  - School zone warning lights are **flashing**, OR
  - **Children are present** near the road (even outside of school hours)
- Typical school zone speed: 20 mph (varies; follow posted sign)
- Reduced speed applies regardless of whether school is in session

## Work/Construction Zones
- Slow to the posted work zone speed limit
- **Fines doubled** for speeding in active work zones when workers are present (Texas Transportation Code)
- Stay alert for flaggers, lane shifts, uneven pavement, and workers on foot
- Work zone speed limits are in effect 24/7 unless posted otherwise

## Following Distance
- Minimum recommended following distance: **3 seconds** under normal conditions
- Measurement: when the car ahead passes a fixed point, count 3 seconds before you reach the same point
- Increase to **4–6 seconds** in adverse conditions (rain, fog, ice, heavy traffic)
- **Large vehicles (trucks, buses):** need more following distance to stop
- Tailgating (following too closely) is a major cause of rear-end collisions

## Stopping Distance
- **Total stopping distance = reaction distance + braking distance**
- Reaction distance: average reaction time is 3/4 second
- At 30 mph: stopping distance ≈ 75 feet
- At 55 mph: stopping distance ≈ 228 feet
- At 70 mph: stopping distance ≈ 387 feet
- Doubling speed roughly **quadruples** braking distance (kinetic energy ∝ v²)

## Night Driving
- Headlights illuminate approximately **350–500 feet** ahead
- At highway speed, you need more distance to stop than headlights illuminate ("overdriving headlights")
- Reduce speed at night so you can stop within the lit distance
- Default nighttime highway speed: 65 mph (vs. 70 mph daytime)
- Dim headlights (switch to low beams) when within 500 feet of oncoming vehicle, or within 300 feet following another vehicle

## Adverse Weather Conditions

### Rain
- Reduce speed; increase following distance
- Wet roads can cause hydroplaning
- Turn on headlights when wipers are in use (Texas law)
- Hydroplaning can begin at speeds as low as **35 mph** — easing off the accelerator and steering gently helps recover
- Pump brakes gently to test traction after puddles

### Fog
- Use **low-beam headlights** (high beams reflect off fog, reducing visibility)
- Reduce speed significantly
- Increase following distance
- If visibility becomes dangerously limited: pull completely off the road, turn off headlights, leave hazard lights on

### Ice and Snow
- Black ice: invisible thin ice layer, most common on bridges, overpasses, and shaded areas
- Reduce speed; avoid sudden steering or braking
- If skidding: ease off the gas; steer in the direction you want to go; do not over-correct

### Wind
- Strong crosswinds affect large vehicles most
- Grip the steering wheel firmly; reduce speed

## Special Road Situations

### Curves
- Slow before the curve; do not brake in the middle of a curve
- Stay in your lane; maintain a steady speed through the curve

### Hills
- Do not pass on a hill where you cannot see at least 500 feet ahead
- Gravity increases stopping distance going downhill
- Shift to lower gear to control speed on long downgrades

### Bridges and Overpasses
- Freeze first in cold weather (less thermal mass than road surface)
- No parking on bridges
- Weight limits posted on many rural bridges

### Railroads
- Slow down and look both ways at all railroad crossings
- Stop when lights flash or gates lower — do not try to beat the train
- Stop at least **15 feet** from the nearest rail
- Crossbuck sign = yield; if train is approaching, stop
- Never stop on railroad tracks; if stalled on tracks, get everyone out and move away

### Hydroplaning
- Caused by water building up under tires, breaking traction with road surface
- More likely at higher speeds (35+ mph) and with worn tread
- Recovery: ease off gas, steer straight or gently, avoid hard braking

### Driving Through Water
- Do not drive through flooded roads — "Turn Around, Don't Drown"
- Just 6 inches of fast-moving water can knock a person down; 12 inches can float most vehicles
- If you must go through a shallow puddle, test your brakes afterward
